# Ghana Health Dataset: Comprehensive Analysis Summary

## Executive Overview

This analysis examines health trends in Ghana from 1960 to 2024, covering key indicators including mortality, disease prevalence, healthcare access, nutrition, and population dynamics. The data reveals significant progress in many health outcomes alongside persistent challenges in certain areas.

---

## 1. Major Improvements (Positive Trends)

### 1.1 Child Mortality
- **Under-5 mortality rate** declined from ~120 per 1,000 live births (1990) to ~37 per 1,000 (2023)
- **Infant mortality rate** decreased from ~122 per 1,000 (1960) to ~28 per 1,000 (2023)
- **Neonatal mortality rate** dropped from ~59 per 1,000 (1965) to ~21 per 1,000 (2023)
- **Absolute reduction**: Approximately 69% decrease in under-5 mortality

### 1.2 Maternal Health
- **Maternal mortality ratio** fell from ~770 per 100,000 live births (1990) to ~234 per 100,000 (2023)
- **Reduction of nearly 70%** over three decades
- **Skilled birth attendance** increased from ~40% (1988) to ~79% (2018)
- **Prenatal care coverage** now exceeds 97% of pregnant women

### 1.3 Life Expectancy
- **Overall life expectancy** increased from ~47 years (1960) to ~65.5 years (2023)
- **Female life expectancy** (67.9 years) exceeds male life expectancy (63.1 years)
- **Gain of nearly 20 years** over six decades

### 1.4 Fertility and Population Dynamics
- **Total fertility rate** dropped from ~6.9 births per woman (1970) to ~3.4 (2023)
- **Crude birth rate** decreased from ~50 per 1,000 (1960) to ~26 per 1,000 (2023)
- **Adolescent fertility rate** declined from ~167 to ~58 births per 1,000 women (ages 15-19)

### 1.5 Immunization Coverage
- **DPT immunization** increased from ~58% (1990) to ~95% (2024)
- **Measles immunization** rose from ~61% (1990) to ~90% (2024)
- **Hepatitis B3 vaccination** achieved 95% coverage (2024)
- **Newborns protected against tetanus** increased from ~60% (1990) to ~90% (2024)

---

## 2. Disease Trends

### 2.1 HIV/AIDS
- **HIV prevalence** peaked around 2.6% (2000) and declined to 1.5% (2024)
- **Women's share** of people living with HIV increased from ~59% (1990) to ~70% (2024)
- **Antiretroviral therapy coverage** improved from 0% (2000) to 47% (2024)
- **New HIV infections** decreased from ~29,000 (1990) to ~15,000 (2024)

### 2.2 Malaria
- **Malaria incidence** peaked at ~430 per 1,000 (2000) and declined to ~196 per 1,000 (2024)
- **Insecticide-treated bed net use** among under-5 children increased from ~4% (2003) to ~54% (2019)
- **Intermittent preventive treatment** for pregnant women reached 61% (2019)

### 2.3 Tuberculosis
- **TB incidence** decreased from ~216 per 100,000 (2000) to ~126 per 100,000 (2024)
- **Treatment success rate** improved from 50% (2000) to 90% (2023)
- **Case detection rate** remains low at 46% (2024), indicating under-diagnosis

---

## 3. Persistent Challenges

### 3.1 Nutrition
- **Anemia in children** (6-59 months): 59.5% (2019) - remains very high
- **Anemia in women** (15-49 years): 36% (2023) - only modest improvement from 47% (2000)
- **Anemia in pregnant women**: 45.3% (2023) - persistently high
- **Stunting** in children under 5: 17.4% (2022) - improving but still significant
- **Underweight children**: 12% (2022) - moderate improvement from 25% (1993)
- **Vitamin A supplementation** coverage fluctuates around 40-50%

### 3.2 Healthcare Infrastructure
- **Physician density**: Only 0.27 per 1,000 people (2023) - below WHO recommendation
- **Hospital beds**: Declined from ~1.5 per 1,000 (1990) to ~0.7 per 1,000 (2015)
- **Nurses and midwives**: 4.11 per 1,000 people (2023) - improving but still insufficient
- **Health expenditure**: ~3% of GDP (2023) - relatively low by international standards

### 3.3 Water and Sanitation
- **Rural water access**: Only 76% have basic drinking water services (2024)
- **Rural sanitation access**: Only 23% have basic sanitation services (2024)
- **Urban-rural gap**: 23% gap for water, 14% gap for sanitation
- **Open defecation**: Still practiced by ~18% of the population (2024)
- **Safely managed water services**: Only 43% of population (2024)

### 3.4 Cause of Death Shift
- **Communicable diseases** declined from 64% (2000) to 45% (2019) of deaths
- **Non-communicable diseases** increased from 29% (2000) to 45% (2019) of deaths
- **Double burden** of disease now evident in Ghana

### 3.5 Gender-Based Issues
- **Child marriage** (by age 18): 16.1% (2022) - improving from 41% (1988)
- **Teenage pregnancy**: 12.7% (2022) - modest decline from 23% (1988)
- **Belief in wife beating** (any reason): 19.3% (2022) - significant decline from 48.5% (2003)
- **Female-headed households**: 36.6% (2022)

---

## 4. Healthcare Access Barriers

### 4.1 Financial Barriers
- **Getting money for treatment** remains the top barrier, affecting 23-59% of women depending on wealth quintile
- **Out-of-pocket expenditure** accounts for ~27% of current health expenditure (2023)
- **Risk of catastrophic expenditure** for surgical care: 9.1% (2022)

### 4.2 Geographic Barriers
- **Distance to health facility** affects 13-45% of women in poorest quintile
- **Rural populations** face significantly greater access challenges

### 4.3 Social Barriers
- **Not wanting to go alone** affects 12-24% of women
- **Getting permission** remains a barrier for 4-8% of women

---

## 5. Population Structure

### 5.1 Age Distribution (2024)
- **0-14 years**: 35.8% of population (young population)
- **15-64 years**: 60.5% of population (working age)
- **65+ years**: 3.7% of population (aging population)
- **Dependency ratio**: 65.4% (declining from 93.5% in 1960)

### 5.2 Gender Distribution
- **Female population**: 50.06% (slight majority)
- **Sex ratio at birth**: 1.038 males per female (stable)

### 5.3 Migration
- **Net migration** varies significantly year to year
- **International migrants**: ~1.5% of population
- **Recent trend**: Net negative migration (more leaving than arriving)

---

## 6. Health Expenditure Trends

### 6.1 Spending Patterns
- **Current health expenditure** as % of GDP: 2.9% (2023) - down from peak of 4.6% (2013)
- **Per capita health expenditure**: $70 (2023) - up from $10 (2000)
- **Government health expenditure**: 63% of current health expenditure (2023)
- **External health expenditure**: 8.5% of current health expenditure (2023)

### 6.2 PPP Comparison
- **Health expenditure per capita (PPP)** : $234 (2023)
- **Significant increase** from $44 (2000)

---

## 7. Key Recommendations

Based on the findings, the following priority areas emerge:

### Immediate Priorities
1. **Reduce anemia** through supplementation and nutrition programs
2. **Improve physician density** and healthcare worker training
3. **Expand rural water and sanitation** infrastructure
4. **Increase health expenditure** to at least 5% of GDP

### Medium-Term Priorities
1. **Address non-communicable diseases** as they become leading causes of death
2. **Reduce teenage pregnancy** through comprehensive sex education
3. **Improve TB case detection** rates
4. **Expand ART coverage** for HIV to reach 90% target

### Long-Term Priorities
1. **Prepare for aging population** and associated healthcare needs
2. **Reduce urban-rural health disparities**
3. **Strengthen health insurance** to reduce out-of-pocket expenditure
4. **Invest in preventive care** to reduce disease burden

---

## 8. Conclusion

Ghana has made remarkable progress in child survival, maternal health, life expectancy, and infectious disease control over the past six decades. The country has successfully transitioned from high to moderate mortality rates and has seen significant improvements in healthcare access and immunization coverage.

However, persistent challenges remain in nutrition (particularly anemia), healthcare infrastructure (especially physician density), water and sanitation access in rural areas, and the emerging burden of non-communicable diseases. The urban-rural gap in health services remains substantial, and financial barriers to healthcare access continue to affect vulnerable populations.

Continued investment in primary healthcare, nutrition programs, health workforce development, and infrastructure will be essential for Ghana to achieve universal health coverage and address the double burden of communicable and non-communicable diseases.

## 9. Data Quality Notes

- **Data sources**: WHO, UN Population Division, UNICEF, UNAIDS
- **Time coverage**: 1960-2024 (varies by indicator)
- **Gaps exist** for some indicators in early years
- **Modeled estimates** used for some indicators (e.g., maternal mortality)
- **Survey-based data** for many household-level indicators
